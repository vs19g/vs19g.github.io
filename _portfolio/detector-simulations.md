Before cutting metal or stringing wires in the machine shop, a deep understanding of detector physics is required to optimize signal-to-noise ratios and charge collection efficiency. I utilize a fully open-source computational stack to model detector behavior from first principles.

### Geometry Meshing, Electric Fields, & Visualization (Gmsh, Elmer, & ParaView)
To generate precise electrostatic maps of our custom gas-filled detectors, I built a finite-element pipeline:
* **Gmsh:** I design the 3D detector geometries and generate high-quality, dense computational meshes, paying special attention to regions near fine anode wires where the field gradients are extreme.
* **Elmer FEM:** I solve the electrostatic equations across the Gmsh mesh to map the internal electric fields. 
* **ParaView:** I utilize ParaView to process and render high-fidelity 3D visualizations of the Elmer output. This allows me to qualitatively assess field uniformities, identify potential sparking/breakdown regions, and optimize cathode structures across the active tracking area before exporting the field maps.

### Electron Drift & Avalanche (Garfield++)
To understand the dynamic response of our proportional counters, I interface the Elmer electric field maps with **Garfield++** (integrated via **ROOT** in C++). 
* I simulate the primary ionization tracks of incident heavy ions.
* By modeling the electron drift velocities, diffusion, and Townsend avalanche processes within the imported electric field, I can predict the theoretical gain and signal timing of the physical detector before it is built.

### Energy Loss & Kinematics (pycatima / C++)
Predicting how different ion species interact with our active gas target is critical for planning experiments.
* I write custom C++ and Python scripts utilizing **pycatima** to calculate the stopping power and energy loss straggling of rare-isotope beams as they traverse the detector gas.
* These kinematic simulations dictate the precise gas pressures and beam energies required to center the $(\alpha,p)$ reaction vertex within the acceptance of the ANASEN silicon array.
